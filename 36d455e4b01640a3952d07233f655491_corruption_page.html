Combating Corruption and Defending Democracy

Combating corruption is a key pillar of defending American democracy, as highlighted in the Brookings Democracy Playbook 2025. If unchecked, corruption threatens the U.S. economy, security, and democracy.

The new administration has moved quickly to undermine key U.S. anti-corruption protections and guardrails across government.

Businesses, policymakers, civil society, media, and citizens have tools to defend against a potentially devastating wave of corruption and other threats to democratic governance.

Introduction

Corruption is a persistent threat to business integrity and economic growth. As companies expand globally, understanding how corruption influences business operations is essential. The Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI), developed by Transparency International, is a benchmark tool for measuring public sector corruption worldwide.

The Corruption Perceptions Index

Figure 1: Corruption Perceptions Index 2023 (Transparency International)

The CPI is a globally recognized tool that provides insights not only into governance but also into economic risk and opportunity. It is particularly useful for multinational corporations seeking to assess political risk before entering emerging markets.

The CPI ranks 180 countries and territories by perceived levels of public sector corruption, using a scale from 0 (highly corrupt) to 100 (very clean). The 2023 data shows over two-thirds of countries score below 50, highlighting the global scope of the issue.

Impacts on Business Operations

Figure 2: Corruption influences global supply chains and investment decisions.

Institutional corruption discourages foreign direct investment (FDI) and often leads to inefficient public spending. Companies must weigh both ethical and practical risks before engaging in high-risk jurisdictions.

High corruption levels lead to unpredictable regulations, bribery, and increased operational costs. It affects location selection, supply chain transparency, and legal compliance. Businesses must assess corruption risk when entering new markets or managing third-party partnerships.

Environmental and Social Impact

Figure 3: Corruption undermines climate policy and sustainability efforts.

Environmental governance is especially vulnerable in corrupt systems. Laws may exist on paper but fail in implementation due to bribery or political capture.

Corruption weakens environmental protections, disrupts sustainable development, and fosters inequality. Misappropriation of public funds meant for green initiatives delays critical infrastructure projects and hinders international climate goals.

Corporate Governance and Anti-Corruption Measures

Governance structures that emphasize transparency can help firms prevent violations of international laws, such as the U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA). Internal audits and ethics training further reinforce organizational integrity.

Organizations are increasingly adopting anti-corruption frameworks such as ISO 37001 and implementing due diligence mechanisms. Internal policies include whistleblower systems, third-party audits, and global ethics training programs to uphold accountability and compliance.

Conclusion

The CPI provides invaluable insight for decision-making in global operations. Companies that actively assess and manage corruption risks are better equipped to operate ethically and sustainably across international borders. Understanding corruption trends is fundamental to operational resilience and long-term success.

Corruption’s Impact on Operations

Understanding how corruption affects business operations is important for making smart decisions in a global economy. The Corruption Perceptions Index, created by Transparency International, measures how corrupt the public sector is believed to be in countries around the world. This paper explains why the index is useful for professionals in operations management, describes how it is created, explores how it impacts business location decisions, and reflects on insights from the Transparency International website. Learning about this index helps companies reduce risk, act ethically, and plan their global strategies more effectively.

The annual Corruption Perceptions Index is a helpful tool in the field of operations management. It provides a global overview of how people view public sector corruption in 180 countries and territories. For professionals working in operations, this index helps identify potential problems when planning supply chains, choosing suppliers, or deciding where to open new facilities around the world.

The Corruption Perceptions Index gives each country a score from zero to one hundred. It uses information from thirteen different sources, including large international organizations and private consulting firms. These sources examine things like how common bribery is, whether public money is used properly, and how strong a country’s anti-corruption systems are. The most recent report showed that forty-seven countries received their lowest scores since the current scoring system began, showing that corruption is still a major issue in many areas.

Corruption levels can strongly affect where companies choose to operate. Countries with high levels of corruption can create serious challenges, such as unfair rules, unexpected costs, and legal risks. For example, corruption can hurt global efforts to fight climate change. In corrupt countries, funding meant for environmental projects might be stolen, or powerful groups might prevent important environmental policies from being passed. On the other hand, countries with high scores and low corruption are considered better places for businesses because their systems are more fair, stable, and predictable.

While exploring the Transparency International website, one surprising fact was how widespread corruption still is around the world. More than two-thirds of the countries had scores below fifty, and some regions had especially low averages. It was also surprising to see how much corruption affects environmental efforts, showing that corruption is not just a business issue—it also has global consequences for climate and sustainability.

In conclusion, the Corruption Perceptions Index is an important resource for anyone involved in operations management. It gives a clear picture of the level of corruption in different countries and helps companies avoid serious risks. By understanding where corruption is most common, operations managers can make better decisions about where to do business. The index also shows that corruption can have broader impacts, such as stopping progress on important issues like climate change. Staying informed about global corruption trends is essential for responsible and successful operations.
